Job Market Paper

Solar Tariffs and the Energy Transition: Clean-Investment Dynamics and Climate Policy Responses in the Global Economy

Abstract

Trade protection can support domestic clean-energy manufacturing, but the same products are also capital goods needed for decarbonization. This paper studies that tension in the context of U.S. tariffs on imported solar modules. The central mechanism is that tariffs expand domestic module production while raising the cost of renewable investment, slowing clean-capacity accumulation and increasing emissions. Because countries adjust climate policy and the effects spill across trade partners and the global climate, the domestic gains from protection must be weighed against delayed decarbonization and cross-country welfare losses. The results show that U.S. welfare is hump-shaped in the tariff: moderate protection can be beneficial, but higher tariffs increasingly undermine the energy transition.

Working Papers

The Long-term Effects of the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism

With Yongyang Cai.

Abstract

This paper develops a 13-region dynamic spatial integrated assessment model that combines forward-looking investment, sector-level bilateral trade, endogenous climate dynamics, and climate–economy feedbacks to quantify the long-run effects of the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism. CBAM modestly reduces global emissions under baseline coverage, but its effects become substantially stronger when capital adjusts endogenously and when more countries and sectors adopt similar mechanisms. The gains are concentrated in developed economies, while developing regions bear larger welfare losses and much of the mitigation burden.

Rising Temperatures, Structural Transformation, and Within-Country Wage Inequality

Abstract

This paper combines individual-level panel data from the China Family Panel Studies with county-level weather data to examine how rising temperatures affect the skill premium. A 1°C increase in annual average temperature lowers the skill premium by approximately 1 percentage point and is accompanied by a 2.4-percentage-point shift in employment from the tertiary to the secondary sector. The results suggest that temperature-induced structural transformation toward more climate-exposed and less skill-intensive sectors is an important mechanism.

Throw a Bunch Away: A Bundled Utility Maximization Framework Modeling Multiple-Item Food Discarding

With Wuyang Hu, Xiaolei Li, Jian Li, and Ping Qing.

Abstract

This paper develops and empirically tests a bundled utility maximization framework for household decisions to discard multiple near-expiration food items. We find significant, non-monotonic bundle effects: after controlling for food attributes, the additional utility is positive for discarding one item or all items, but negative for discarding exactly two. These findings imply that food-waste interventions targeting individual products may mispredict household behavior unless they account for the combinations of foods households face.
